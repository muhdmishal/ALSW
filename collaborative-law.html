What is Collaborative Law?

Collaborative Law originated in the United States and was launched in England and Wales in 2003.

It is a way of sorting out issues relating to finances or children on divorce, through face to face meetings between the parties and their specially trained collaborative lawyers.

The process allows you to go at your own pace, rather than as dictated by the court. It is much more informal and gives you the opportunity to put forward your own wishes rather than doing so through a representative, yet at the same time you will have the benefit of your own independent lawyer to advise you in relation to legal issues throughout the process. It will be you and your partner/spouse who decide the outcome, not a lawyer or a judge.

Negotiations take place at a number of face to face meetings between the parties and their respective collaborative lawyers. At the first meeting each party signs a Participation Agreement confirming their commitment to the process. The parties also set a timetable for disclosure, experts and subsequent meetings. There may be as few as one or two meetings or there may be up to four or five, depending upon each particular case.

Once agreement is reached the collaborative lawyers prepared any necessary documentation required to give effect to the agreement.

Does collaborative process work for everyone?

The collaborative process is usually best suited to those who are genuinely prepared to work together to resolve issues that arise in the course of negotiations and who are prepared to be open and frank in providing information about their assets and personal circumstances. It would not work well where one party is much more dominant than the other or where one party is intimidated by the other.

Why could the collaborative process work better for me?

The process is usually less costly and can be much quicker, providing negotiations do not breakdown, because you are not constrained by a court timetable. It is much less confrontational than the court route and it saves the distress and upset which usually results when there are court proceedings.

If there are children your involvement with each other will not end on divorce/separation the collaborative process helps prevent the damage caused by bitter disputes which inevitably impact on the children. The process also helps to encourage, from the very start, a relationship of compromise and fairness in the future.

What is the difference between collaborative law, mediation and arbitration?

A mediator’s role, as a neutral third party, is not to give legal advice, but to help you resolve issues that arise between you so that there is no need to involve the court.

If agreement is reached at mediation you will be advised by the mediator to seek independent legal advice. The mediator does not prepare papers/documents for you, this will be done by the independent legal advisers.

In the collaborative process, each party has their own separate lawyer, who will advise throughout and who will prepare any necessary papers as part of the process.

Arbitration is another way of resolving family disputes without the need to go to court. It is cost effective and quick. Family arbitration can be used to resolve disputes relating to finances, property and child maintenance, but not for child arrangement orders. Both parties select a family arbitrator who has the appropriate specialism to deal with the case. The arbitrator will make a decision fairly and impartially for you, based on the information given to them, both parties having agreed to be bound by the decision. You can involve your own lawyer in the process and unlike mediation and collaborative law, which can result in a breakdown of negotiations, a decision is made on your behalf creating finality.
